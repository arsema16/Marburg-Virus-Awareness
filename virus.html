What is Marburg virus?

Marburg virus causes a severe and often fatal hemorrhagic fever in humans. It belongs to the filovirus family, related to Ebola. Fruit bats are considered the natural reservoir, and the virus can transmit to humans and then spread between people through direct contact with bodily fluids or contaminated materials.

Key facts

  • Family: Filoviridae (related to Ebola)
  • Disease: Marburg virus disease (MVD)
  • Onset: Typically 2–21 days after exposure
  • Care: Early supportive care; seek medical attention urgently

How it spreads

Animal → human

Transmission from infected animals, particularly fruit bats or non‑human primates; exposure in bat‑inhabited caves or mines.

Human → human

Through direct contact with blood, secretions, organs, or other body fluids; via contaminated surfaces like bedding or equipment.

After recovery

Virus can persist in some body fluids (e.g., semen) for a period; safe practices remain important.

Symptoms

Early

  • High fever
  • Intense headache
  • Severe fatigue/weakness
  • Muscle aches
  • Abdominal pain
  • Nausea/vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Red eyes; skin rash

Progression

  • Internal and external bleeding
  • Bleeding from gums; nosebleeds
  • Blood in vomit or stool
  • Organ failure; shock

Act quickly

  • Seek urgent medical care
  • Isolate and avoid close contact
  • Use protective measures if caring

Prevention

There is no approved vaccine or specific medicine for MVD. Prevention focuses on avoiding exposure and reducing transmission risk.

Avoid high‑risk areas

Limit entry to bat‑inhabited caves or mines without protection.

Use protective gear

Wear gloves, masks, and protective clothing when caring for the sick.

Hygiene

Wash hands often; avoid sharing sharp objects or personal items.

Safe practices

Cook meat properly; avoid touching bodies during funeral rituals.

Report quickly

Report suspected illnesses to health officials immediately.
